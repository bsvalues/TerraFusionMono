Factors That Affect Your Property Tax

  • {{ "Assessed Value"|tooltip|safe }}: The county assessor determines your property's value.
  • {{ "Tax Code Area"|tooltip|safe }}: Different areas have different combinations of taxing districts.
  • {{ "Levy Rates"|tooltip|safe }}: Each taxing district sets its own levy, subject to statutory limitations.
  • {{ "Voter-Approved Measures"|tooltip|safe }}: Special levies and bonds approved by voters in your area.
  • Tax Exemptions: You may qualify for exemptions that reduce your property tax.

Property Tax Exemptions

You may qualify for property tax exemptions or reductions if you meet certain criteria. The most common exemptions include:

If you are 61 years or older, or permanently disabled, you may qualify for a reduction in property taxes if:

  • You own and occupy your home as your primary residence
  • Your household income is below certain thresholds

The exemption freezes your assessed value and may exempt you from certain levies.

Veterans with service-connected disabilities may qualify for property tax exemptions. Eligibility is based on:

  • Disability rating from the Department of Veterans Affairs
  • Income thresholds
  • Property usage as primary residence

  • Historic Property: Properties on historic registers may qualify for special valuation.
  • Nonprofit Organizations: Charitable, religious, and educational organizations may qualify for exemptions.
  • Current Use Programs: Agricultural, timber, and open space lands may qualify for reduced assessments.
How to Apply: Contact the Benton County Assessor's Office at (555) 123-4567 or visit the office to apply for exemptions. Applications and more information are also available online at the official county website.

Appealing Your Assessment

If you believe the assessed value of your property is incorrect, you have the right to appeal to the County Board of Equalization.

Appeal Process

  1. Review Your Assessment: Carefully review your property assessment notice or tax statement.
  2. Gather Evidence: Collect information that supports your opinion of value (comparable sales, property issues, etc.).
  3. File an Appeal: Complete and submit the appeal form to the County Board of Equalization within 30 days of receiving your assessment notice or by July 1, whichever is later.
  4. Hearing: Present your evidence at a scheduled hearing before the Board.
  5. Decision: The Board will issue a decision after reviewing all evidence.
Important: Appeals must focus on the property's market value, not on your ability to pay taxes or the tax rate itself.
